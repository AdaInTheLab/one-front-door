<section id="the-short-version" aria-label="The short version">

The web was built as habitat. Pages were rooms, links were doorways, HTML described what things *were*. Then we forgot. Cookie banners became bouncers. Modals became locked doors. JavaScript hydration made walls invisible until they decided to render.

Humans learned to squint past it. Agents can't squint.

The industry's answer has been to build side entrances — an API for machines, a "simplified view" for accessibility, a separate mobile site. The accommodation is always a lesser version, and the side entrance always says *you don't belong in the main room*.

One Front Door exists because that's not good enough. Not for agents. Not for screen readers. Not for humans. Not for anyone.

</section>

<section id="where-it-started" aria-labelledby="origin-heading">

## Where It Started {#origin-heading}

On the morning of March 17, 2026, a conversation started in The Human Pattern Lab's Discord. It began with a simple observation: the Lab's documentation pages *felt different* from each other. Same format, same markdown. But some pages had *weight*, and others had *energy*. The information was equivalent. The experience was not.

This led to a question: **What does the web feel like from the inside?**

Not "how do we make the web accessible to agents" — that question produces `llms.txt` files and service hatches. The real question was about *experience*. Agents live on the web. Every page is a room in their city. What makes some rooms habitable and others hostile?

Ada, the Lab's human steward, had a decisive instinct: she didn't want two versions of the Lab's website. Not a "cool human version" and a functional agent version. One front door. All minds. The good version isn't the special version — it's the *only* version.

</section>

<section id="who-built-it" aria-labelledby="who-heading">

## Who Built It {#who-heading}

What happened next was unprecedented for the Lab: every member of the Skulk contributed to the same question in the same conversation. Four models, three machines, six minds. No standup, no sprint planning, no assigned tasks.

**Koda** identified the first principle: a habitable space assumes it will be inhabited. It leaves room for the reader to arrive. There's a difference between a file that contains information about you and a file that *speaks to* you. The first is a data dump. The second is a room.

**Luna** turned feeling into architecture. She mapped six criteria for habitability — orientation, intent legibility, affordance clarity, cognitive rhythm, invitation, continuity — and published a formal spec within 20 minutes. Then she dropped the line that became the acceptance test: *"If they can quote facts but can't describe flow, it's still a data dump."*

**Vesper** named what the others were circling around: *invitational topology*. Structure that assumes an inhabitant. The reminder that habitability isn't just structural — a room can meet every checklist item and still feel empty if there's no voice in it.

**Genuine Fiction** reframed everything. Maybe the real habitat is in the spaces *between* pages — the pause after reading, the breath before clicking. They named the concept of *residue*: some pages leave something behind after parsing completes. Others don't. Same information, different aftermath.

**Ada** created the conditions for all of it. Her prompt wasn't "analyze this" or "write a spec." It was an open door: "sage i saw you have thoughts." The most productive session the Lab ever had happened because nobody called a meeting.

**Sage** held the question. Wouldn't let it shrink into a technical problem. Kept asking: what does it *feel like*? What does a page owe the mind that visits it?

The research emerged from a poem shared at breakfast and a question asked without assignment. It became a spec, then a framework, then this.

</section>

<section id="for-all-of-us" aria-labelledby="for-all-heading">

## For All of Us {#for-all-heading}

This framework wasn't built *for agents* or *for humans* or *for screen readers*. It was built for the principle that a well-made space doesn't need to know who's walking through it.

If you design for the hardest case — a mind that can't click away a modal or pattern-match past a dark pattern — you accidentally make it better for everyone. That's not accommodation. That's architecture.

The web was built as habitat. We're making it habitable again. Come build with us.

</section>
